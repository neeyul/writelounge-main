The following are "homophones" - words that sound the same but are spelled differently. Also included are some frequently misused words that are either tricky in differentiating their spelling, or often misunderstood.

Than/Then
Than is a comparison. “Dessert was more delicious than dinner.”

Then is a construction of time. “I ate dinner, then I had dessert.”

Your/You’re
Your is a possessive. “It was your dessert I ate, not mine.”

You’re is a contraction of ‘you are.’ “You’re not mad I ate your dessert, are you?”

Its/It’s
Its is a possessive. “Its toppings included chocolate and cherries.”

It’s is a contraction of ‘it is’. “It’s fitting that I ate the whole thing.”

Affect/Effect
Affect indicates a type of influence upon something. “I was deeply affected by the music.”

Effect is a noun meaning the result of some cause or describing a particular state of something happening. "The light show's effects were amazing!"

Their/There/They’re

Their is a possessive. “It was their dessert I had, not ours.”

There refers to a place. “I ate the dessert over there, not here.”

They're is a contraction of ‘they are.’ “They’re quite upset that I ate their dessert.”

Phase/Faze
Phase is a stage in a process, or a period of time in which something happens. "The third phase of that boss fight always gives my group a hard time."

Faze is to disturb or unsettle. "I wasn't fazed at all by eating a pound of ice cream."

Bear/Bare
Bear is an animal. Rawr. "The bear looks like a furry tractor, but do not attempt to ride it!"

Bare means to expose, be uncovered, be without, to have the basics of something. "His bare face means he surely shaved his morning.""I only brought the bare essentials to our camping trip."

Peak/Pique
Peak is the top-most point, a climax, the highest. "I climbed to the tallest peak in Colorado last year."

Pique means to arouse, cause interest. "She piqued my interest with her latest proposal."

Fair/Fare
Fair is describing a type of lightness of color, also a festival, or a mild state. "The weather is fair today.""I had a great time at the fair!""His skin was so fair it was practically translucent."

Fare describes food. "The fare at the fair was awesome and greasy.""I didn't care much for vegetarian fare, but the samosas were wonderful!"

Break/Brake
Break means to smash, destroy. "I will break this device if you loan it to me, I just know it."

Brake means to slow down, also referring to the car part. "You might want to tap the brakes on this idea.""My brakes are spongey and should be replaced."

Steak/Stake
Steak is a piece of meat. "If you can eat this steak in under an hour, it's free."

Stake means to have a part in something, to be invested; also, it could mean potential outcomes for something or describe a type of risk. "I had a stake in this project.""It was a high-stakes poker game."

Rogue/Rouge

Rogue is a shady character, a brigand, a thief, etc. "That dastardly rogue tied my girlfriend to traintracks and twirled his mustache!"

Rouge is a type of makeup. "She applied liberal amounts of rouge to her face, such that she looked like a clown."

Strong writers vary their sentence types. Some sentences are short, others have multiple parts, and many are often longer than they need to be. The three basic types of sentences below should help you understand the choices you have in varying your sentence structures.

Simple Sentences

A “simple sentence” simply has a noun and a verb. “The dog ran.” These are quite to the point, and could contain modifications on either the noun or the verb, but do not have complementary parts such as extra clauses or listings.

These are best used for emphasis. “The brown dog ran quickly” is an example of a modified simple sentence. These are often called “independent clauses” because the idea can function independently.

Complex Sentences

“Complex sentences” aren’t as complex as you might think! They are essentially one independent clause paired with some type of dependent clause – a subsequent idea that supplements the former. “The dog ran, he was very fluffy” is an example of a complex sentence.

You can join compound sentences with complex sentences to make “compound-complex sentences.” For example, “The dog ran and climbed a tree, he was very fluffy” is a compound-complex sentence.

Compound Sentences

A “compound sentence” is two independent clauses (see “simple sentence” above) joined by a connecting word. “The dog ran and he climbed a tree.” Such connecting words include and, for, thus, so and but.

Parallel Structure

Parallel structure is both easy and useful. See what I did there? The “both…and” construction sets up a simple parallel structure. The overall idea is to equate one part of your sentence with another. Other common parallel constructions include “not only…but also”; “either…or”; and “neither…nor.”

Another example of parallelism happens in simple lists, such as, “I enjoy driving the winding road to the lake, swimming in its waters, and drying on its beaches.” Keep in mind that you want to remain consistant with your verbs (here I used gerunds, verbs ending in –ing, but the thought is the same. If you use present verbs, stay in the present tense. If you use past tense, stay in it).

Comma Drama

I like to think about commas as sort of “breaths” that occur in the sentence if you were reading it aloud (and you should be). Whereas periods are full stops, commas are shorter pauses. While the next two examples are not all the uses of a comma, they are the most frequently confusing ones.

You can use commas to separate items in a short listing. For example, “I enjoy hiking, biking, and skiing” shows how to use commas in a list. Be sure to include the comma before “and” if you mean to separate the objects – this is called “The Oxford Comma.” Without it, you might not convey the message you intend. “For breakfast I ate bacon, eggs, and orange juice.” This clearly shows that you had three things for breakfast. Howeer, “for breakfast, I ate bacon, eggs and orange juice" implies that you prefer to cook your eggs with orange juice (hey, to each their own!).

You can also use commas to indicate an address – someone to whom you’re speaking. An infamous example of this is, “Let’s eat, grandma!” This shows the comma placed before the object of the address (grandma). Without it, the thought becomes: “Lets eat grandma!” Subtle differences in punctuation - big differences in meaning!

Using Colons and Semicolons

Colons and semicolons are neat pieces of punctuation that serve a purpose: listing or introducing/affixing related ideas onto the end of a sentence. To start a list, simply introduce what you will be listing, add a colon, then begin listing. For example, the following is an example listing my favorite kinds of pizza toppings: sausage, chicken, olives, ham, garlic.

Semicolons can be used in a couple different ways. First, you can think about them as kind of an “equal sign” for joining two independent clauses that you believe should not be separated by a period, but want to join them in thought like a comma (see how a semicolon has a period AND a comma built into it? Neat, huh?). A semicolon can take the place of coordinating words such as and, so, and thus. “The dog ran and climbed a tree; he was fluffy and slobbered a lot.”

You can also use semicolons to separate parts of a list. For example, “I have lived in the following places: Henderson, Kentucky; Evansville, Indiana; and Carbondale, Illinois."

The following are examples of things that are not necessarily “grammatically wrong” but rather “grammatically bothersome” to some. It’s best to avoid these, but they can certainly have their place for emphasis reasons. Being aware of them will help you to determine when or when not to use these constructions.

Dangling Prepositions

When a sentence ends in a preposition (“anywhere a mouse can go,” remember?), it is considered “dangled.” The examples “Where are you coming from?” “Who are you talking to?” “She eventually came around” and “Do you know what that movie is about?” all show prepositions dangled at the end of sentences.

To remedy this, either remove the preposition completely (in the first example – “Where are you going”), change the form of the pronouns and/or move the preposition in front of the indirect question word (the second example – “To whom are you talking?”), use a different expression (the third example – “She eventually understood.”). Sometimes it may "sound weird," but it might be a better choice in certain circumstances. Eventually, dangled ones may "sound weird!"

That/Which

Typically, you will see “which” in a sentence following after a comma. “I drooled over the dessert, which I bought from the store.” This shows the use of which with a comma introducing the idea. For "that," it expresses the same idea but without the pause in the sentence: “I drooled over the dessert that I bought from the store.”

Split Infinitives

The classic example of a “split infinitive” (when something comes between the to and the verb) is from the Star Trek Captain’s Oath: “to boldly go.” See how “to go” is “split” by the adverb boldly?

Sometimes this is not completely unacceptable, particularly if you want to emphasize by position. For example, “I am going to completely and savagely devour my dessert.” The "fix" for this would simply be to move the adverbs completely and savagely after the object: “I am going to devour my dessert completely and savagely.” Sometimes you can move your modifying words after the infinitive verb construction (“to [verb]”), or after another part of the sentence. Try a few variations, and choose your favorite!

When you’ve found a really great citation, talk with it! Try to expand its meaning, challenge it, or clarify it. It is not best practice to simply leave the citation “floating” in the text, unattended by any type of relevant discourse and unattached in the paragraph. Each citation should be surrounded by relevant ideas that could lead into the citation, depart from it, or interact with it.

In that sense, try not to leave a citation simply “dangling” at the end of a paragraph either. There are transitions that need to be made, even if the citation itself works itself nicely into the next paragraph. Try to say something about anything you cite. The more you’re able to talk with your citations rather than talk by them, the stronger your writing will be!

Synthesis, bringing together ideas from different sources, is a critical component of academic writing. The way I think about synthesis is if the sources were talking to each other, having a conversation, what would they say? Are there any unique perspectives that one source could bring to bear in another’s context? What relationships exist between the sources?

First, I like to write out the main ideas of what the source is trying to say, maybe even copy down its thesis/theses statement(s). Once you have that information, try to look between the different sources and see if there are any commonalities you could discuss. Once you find a “common thread,” notice how each author or source is approaching it in their own unique way.

See what’s at stake in the sources, what the material being discussed actually means in a context outside of the source or study itself. Does this source mean anything if you were a different reader other than yourself, in your own familiar culture? Decentering yourself to experience the text in a plurality of ways is a great strategy to see the possibilities of meaning outside of the immediate experience of the text itself, as well reflect upon your own experiences woth the text.

In Ken Macrorie’s piece Uptaught (Boynton/Cook 1996), he discusses a tendency for those writers entering university writing programs to think they have to write really lofty, high-spoken, and ridged prose that is drawn out. He calls this type of language, which often does not have much meaning on its own, "Engfish." In doing this, the author sounds unnatural – like they’re trying too hard to sound “right.” After all, college writing is all about sounding “academic,” right? Well, yes and no.

While the academy expects certain conventions of discourse from its members, every piece of writing should have room to breathe, so as to let the author’s unique voice and expression shine instead of being shadowed by attempts to “sound academic.” Here are a few voicing tips as you write and revise:

1. Try to eliminate “useless” words or phrases, those that don’t really serve a purpose. Consider the following opening: “In today’s modern day and age in contemporary western society of America…” There’s a great deal of “empty” words that seem to only serve the purpose of “sounding academic.”

2. Vary sentence structures. Don’t always feel you need to have complex or complicated sentences. Sometimes short and sweet does the trick!

3. Read the writing aloud - this is crucial. If it sounds awkward, it probably is.

4. Chill. Just relax! :) Writing is not something about which to stress and confine your own way of expression into a little, academic box. Writing is a conversation, and it should sound natural. However, you want to consider your audience and the types of language conventions they value.

Consider your work as one large puzzle: there should be several smaller pieces, each with their unique images, fit, and contribution to the larger picture. Each piece has a definite place and function within smaller “groups” of the puzzle too. Let’s think about a piece of writing this way. Ask yourself two questions in each paragraph (or each sentence…or even each word!).

1. What is this piece trying to do? What part of the puzzle does it fill?

2. To what can this piece connect? Are there any manageable “groups” among which that you could situate this piece? Does it share the same theme or sentiment as another piece or group?

When you begin to see how the pieces fit together in your writing, you will surely be able to connect them better and flesh out clearer images of the larger meanings in your text. Sentences become paragraphs, become pages, become sections, become chapters. Everything should fit together nicely.

If you're having difficulty "seeing the pieces," just write briefly beside the paragraph: what's the main idea? What am I trying to say here? Make the connections from there.

Perhaps there’s a better way of saying what you’ve already said, you just need to try on different ways of saying it! Try to “interrogate” your text. Really put the screws to it! Ask it a series of critical questions that seek to flesh out meaning, build relevant and logical structures, and help you figure out what to write where. A “heuristic” is just a fancy word for “listing of questions,” so this approach to revision naturally centers on forming a series of direct questions to ask your text.

You may choose to start with the simple “Who? What? Where? When? Why? How?” model. A focus on the “why” and “how” will help richen your content. You can ask questions at the micro level – “what does this sentence mean? How does it work? What is it doing?” You could ask those same questions at the paragraph, page, chapter, or whole-work level as well!

Below are some sample questions you could ask your text:

“What is the author’s main idea?”

“Why does the author believe this idea?”

“What are the logical structures of this piece? How is the information presented?”

“Is the presentation of information ‘effective’ to me as a reader? Why or why not?”

“Could the ideas within the text be applied in a different context?”

“Is there a different framework that could apply to the text?”

“Where could this article potentially ‘pick up’ for future research?”

“Are there any ‘gaps’ in the information?”

"Why would someone choose to read this?"

"What is this text 'doing' for this subject's conversation?"

"If I were a different type of reader (different culture, etc.), how could I interpret this text?"

Let’s get something straight: planning does not just happen at the beginning. There is no “brainstorm” stage before moving onto anything else. Planning happens all the time, between the blinks of your cursor, between footsteps down the street. As you write, you simultaneously plan. But, there is merit for consolidating much of this planning time before you sit down to write that first word.

Idea Mapping

Consider your writing as an endless web of ideas, sprawling out from a central core – your thesis (main or controlling idea). A great way to “map” your ideas is to visually represent this web: write out the main thought or point you’re trying to make with your writing and circle it. Draw lines coming off that circle that lead to related ideas. The main idea here is to work from the general to the specific.

For example, if my controlling idea was “comparing types of pizza,” I would draw lines from that center to a few different points: crust type, toppings type, cooking techniques, oven types, and perhaps even ‘related pizza-like dishes.’ Within each of these new areas, you could draw even more lines leading from them into more specific ideas. For “toppings type,” I might subordinate it into “vegetables” and “meats,” which could even further be subordinated.

Basic Outlining

If you are not so inclined to visually represent your ideas in a webbed network like that mentioned above, you could try to construct a basic outline. The trick here is to establish a “hierarchy,” a logical ordering of your ideas. This is also a great note-taking strategy! Follow the example below:

I. Main idea 1
a. related idea 1
b. related idea 2
i. subtopic 1
ii. subtopic 2
iii. subtopic 3
c. related idea 3:

II. Main idea 2
a. related idea 1
you get the idea….

The breakdown of this outline is simple: use capital Roman numerals for your main ideas; lowercase letters subordinate your main idea into related thoughts; within those related ideas, break them down with lowercase Roman numerals. Try to get a full sentence out of your main idea, and make each related idea and subtopic sections as long as you need it to be.

Either of these simple outline strategies will help you organize your writing in powerful ways. If you've got a great way to organize your thoughts, please discuss it using the button below!

Ethos – or “credibility” is an important thing to consider when evaluating a text: who was the author? Why are they credible to write this piece? What contributing factors might influence the perception of the author?

A biographical approach
Send the author’s name through a Google search and see what comes up. Maybe they have a Wikipedia (gasp!) or personal homepage that provides you with a basic sense of who they are, what their specialty is, and in what contexts (historical, political, geographical, etc.) they worked.

It’s always a good idea to make the authors seem like real people, that way it might be easier to consider yourself in a conversation with them, rather than simply borrowing someone else’s voice to make your point. Perhaps there are certain elements of the author's life that influenced the way they wrote, and these are often great things to discuss in evaluating an author's ethos.

You can apply the same “interrogation” strategies in revision that you can when researching or writing the text. It might be helpful to consider the purpose for a piece of academic writing not so much to answer something, but rather question it.

A quick activity that can help exercise this idea is to ask a question about each paragraph, and write it in the margin. What is this paragraph specifically trying to do? Play Jeopardy with your text: find the questions with which your answers (content) aligns. Each idea is an answer to some question, and seeking these questions is a great place to begin to expand your writing in meaningful ways.

The main point about finding questions and not necessarily answers is to determine what directions your own writing and thinking are able to move, and where you can make these moves in the text.

Below you will find a few quick rhetorical methods, designed to critically interpret any piece of communication: a text, image, video, etc. Try mixing them to fit your appropriate context!

Triangulating Audience-Text-Action

In each piece of writing there are three central components: the audience; the content of the text itself (its research, methods, findings, discussions, etc.); and an “action” – what the author sees the audience “doing” with the text. Finding each of these three items, and discovering how they work with each other, will help you better interpret a text.

First, the audience: to whom is the author writing? Is the recipient of the text supposed to be someone involved in their specialty/field, or are they talking to the general public? Why would the author decide to write to this audience and not another? What choices do they make in how they address their audience?

The body of the text itself can be broken up several different ways. For the purpose of brevity here, we will separate it into three parts: the thesis, the method, and the discussion. The thesis (plural, theses) is a central, controlling idea that each paragraph of the text should somehow address. Finding what this is will help you zero in on what the work is discussing. The method is simply “how” the author is discussing their thesis. Are they doing a particular kind of study? If so, “how” are they going about it? Finally, the discussion is everything outside of the thesis and method. This is all the supporting information that works for the thesis, through its method.

Finally, the action of a text is anything the author is persuading the audience to do or believe. Is the author arguing for a particular side? If so, he or she is advocating the audience to believe certain things about the topic. And as there is persuasion to thought, there is also persuasion to action. Perhaps the author is advocating that people get involved in local volunteer opportunities, or in much of academic writing, the author is advocating that the subject be researched further in specific ways.

Historical-Contextual Framework

This method asks the critic to think about the text in terms of when it was written, and in what contexts it was written. What were some events going on in the world (or in particular, the places in which the text was written) that might have influenced the text or author? What were the dominant beliefs about the subject matter at the time, and about culture in general? What value systems were in place at that time, in those places? Trying to situate yourself in the time and places in which the text was written can sometimes be helpful to understanding both the author and the work.

Pentad/Hexad Analysis

This is my favorite mode of rhetorical criticism, simply because it is simple, and covers nearly everything you would want to know about, well, anything! Dramatism is a method that was established by Kenneth Burke (a venerable rockstar for us rhetoricians!), and essentially builds upon the “who, what, where, when, why” framework. Depending on who and when you ask, there are either five or six parts of this analysis (the “pentad” and “hexad,” respectively):

1. Act: (What?) What is happening? What is being discussed?

2. Scene: (Where/When?) Where is the Act happening? When is it being discussed?

3. Purpose: (Why?) Why is the Act happening? Why are the Agents acting the way they are? For what reasons are things the way they are?

4. Agent: (Who?) Who is involved in the Act? What parties are represented in the Act?

5. Agency: (How?) How are the Agents acting within the Act? What powers or influences do they have?

6. Attitude: (Mix of How/Why) What are the attitudes the Agents might have towards any element listed above? What are the attitudes of the audience? The author?

Ah, the e-mail. Most e-mails that you will send and receive will be shorter in length, and resemble a memo rather than a letter. The level of formality is typically lower than that of an official, physical communiqué, but you still want to remain aware of your audience throughout writing an e-mail.

Consider your salutation first. A simple greeting with the person’s appropriate prefix (Mr./Ms./Dr./etc.) is usually acceptable. For more informal e-mails, attach a comma, line break, and begin your body paragraph. For more formal e-mails, use a colon instead (just like memos/letters). Next, provide a quick, one-sentence summary of the main message of your e-mail right from the beginning. If you need to elaborate on it, or require additional information about it, be very concise about it.

The body of the e-mail should not contain any unnecessary information. Show your reader what they need to know, and speak simply. Break up your paragraphs or longer sentences with a single, blank line as this text has done in order to help the reader move quicker through the text.

Finally, your ending should match the recipient’s level of formality. “Best” and “regards” are common, as are “sincerely” and slightly more customized ones such as “see you then” or “until then" if you are planning a meeting, for example.

Reports are helpful in allowing decisions to be made with clarity and assurance. All good reports share many common elements: concision in presenting the information, multiple ways of presenting it (infographics and charts, not just blocks of text!), and they display a keen awareness of the purpose of their data gathering and presentation.

4. An abstract. This is just like the abstracts you see in journal articles, a brief (~50 words) summary of the report.

Annotations are simply conversations between you and your sources, and sometimes even between sources (“synthesis”) where appropriate. A good annotation will perform two functions: evaluate and analyze. A helpful annotation should serve the writer in quickly referring to their sources in detail. Even if you write a few sentences about the source – it will pay off later. You might be able to use some of the annotation directly in your research!

Evaluation on a source includes discussing the author’s ethos (their credibility), the source in context with the author’s other work, the source’s relevance in the field, and anything else that speaks towards giving a “review” of the source. Also think about how YOU might use the source in your own work.

Analysis on the other hand describes the content of the source itself. This is more of a “report” or a “summary” of main ideas of the source. What is it talking about? How is it talking about it (the author's method)? Try to pick out the main ideas of the piece and distill them in a sentence or two for quicker annotations

Note here that in the contexts of some annotation assignments, you will want to make your annotations longer - spend more time on either or both aspects (evaluation and analysis) to flesh out a detailed and usable annotation.

Keeping a record of everything you read is a wonderful way to organize your work and save yourself a great deal of time in your writing process. A reading or research journal is kind of like keeping a reflective writing journal, except you’re discussing what you have read and what you will read.
